What is this assignment actually asking you to do?

You work for a local chamber of commerce. A small business owner has come to you and asked you to help them get started on social media. Your job is to:

1Find a real small business and understand what they want to achieve
2Make a plan for how they should use social media
3Get feedback on your plan from the business owner, then improve it
4Actually create the social media accounts and post content
5Collect data and analyse how well your posts performed
6Evaluate everything — what worked, what didn't, and what you'd do differently

Section-by-Section Guide

Click any section to expand it. Every section explained in plain English.

✅ PASS — Sections 1–8

What you're doing: Introducing the business you've chosen to work with.

In plain English: Imagine you're explaining to a stranger what the business is, what it sells, who runs it, and why they need help with social media. Write it simply and clearly.

✓ Business name, type, and owner's contact
✓ How you found/chose this business
✓ What the business currently does online (probably nothing)
✓ What they want to achieve with social media
✓ How social media will help them meet those goals

What you're doing: Creating a detailed plan for the business's social media presence — before you touch anything live.

In plain English: Think of it like planning a trip before you book anything. You decide where you're going (which platforms), who you're going with (target audience), what you'll do (content), and when (posting schedule).

2a — Platform Selection

Choose 3 platforms. Explain why each one is right for this specific business. "It's popular" is not enough — link it to the target audience.

2b — Target Audience

Who are the customers? Age, gender, interests, location. Be specific. "Everyone" is not a target audience.

2c — Keyword Strategy

Think about what someone types into Google when looking for this business. Pick 5 keywords and explain why each one is relevant. Use Google Keyword Planner to back up your choices.

2d — Best Times to Post

Research when your target audience is most active. A bakery audience is different from a gym audience. Find evidence for your timing choices.

2e — Posting Schedule V1

Your original plan. At least 6 posts spread across your 3 platforms over 3 weeks.

2f — Legal & Ethical Considerations

Address copyright, GDPR, advertising standards, appropriate content, and confidentiality. Don't just define them — say what you will actually do about each one.

2g — Social Media Policy

A short set of rules for the business: what they can post, what they must keep private, how to stay secure, and how to keep personal and business accounts separate.

What you're doing: Showing that your plan was reviewed by a real person and improved based on their feedback.

In plain English: You made a plan. Now you take it back to the business owner and ask "does this work for you?" They'll probably have changes. You update the plan, and the key evidence is the before and after.

✓ Evidence of the meeting or contact (notes, email, Google Form)
✓ A table showing what feedback you received and what you changed
✓ Posting Schedule V2 — the improved version with a "what changed" column
⚠️ You must do this BEFORE you start creating posts. The point is to improve the plan, not approve what you've already done.

What you're doing: Setting up the actual social media accounts for the business.

In plain English: Create accounts on all 3 platforms. Think carefully about the username, bio, profile picture, and any links. Screenshot the finished profiles and explain every choice you made.

✓ Account created on all 3 platforms
✓ Screenshot of each profile/bio
✓ Annotation — why that username? Why that bio? Why that image?

What you're doing: Creating and publishing real posts for the business.

In plain English: You're actually posting on the business's social media now. You must use at least 3 different types of content across your 3 platforms. Don't just post 5 photos — mix it up.

Content formats you can use: photos, videos, reels, TikToks, stories, polls, web links, open questions, special offers, behind-the-scenes content.

Three non-negotiables:

✓ Posts published on at least 3 different platforms
✓ At least 3 different content formats used
✓ Every post annotated — why this format? How does it follow the plan?

Complete the Content Matrix in the template first to plan your spread before you start posting.

What you're doing: Showing that you actively engaged with people, not just broadcast posts at them.

In plain English: Social media isn't a one-way street. You need to interact. Reply to comments, ask questions, run a poll, use relevant hashtags to get discovered, set up an auto-reply message.

✓ Replied to at least one comment or message
✓ Created a poll or survey
✓ Used hashtags strategically
✓ Set up an auto-response or CTA button
✓ Screenshot everything as evidence

What you're doing: Collecting and analysing the actual statistics from your posts.

In plain English: Every social media platform shows you data — how many people saw your post, liked it, shared it, and who your audience is. You collect that data and write about what it means.

🚨 Real data only. It does not matter if a post got 1 like. That is your real data and it is fine. Fabricating or estimating statistics will result in a resubmission and a grade cap at Pass — no exceptions.

Where to find your data:

✓ Facebook/Instagram — Meta Business Suite → Insights
✓ TikTok — Creator Tools → Analytics
✓ YouTube — YouTube Studio → Analytics
✓ Twitter/X — Analytics tab on each tweet

What you're doing: Explaining how your social media activity could help the business appear higher in Google search results.

In plain English: SEO means "Search Engine Optimisation" — basically getting Google to notice you. Social media helps with this because when you use the right keywords, post regularly, and get engagement, Google sees the business as more active and relevant.

✓ Explain what SEO is and why it matters for this business
✓ How your keyword strategy supports SEO
✓ How the profile username/URL helps with SEO
✓ Why regular posting matters for search rankings
✓ What you'd recommend to improve SEO further
🔶 MERIT — Sections 9–10

What you're doing: Going back over every major decision in your plan and giving a proper reason why it was right for this specific business.

In plain English: There's a big difference between describing what you did and justifying it. A description says "I chose Instagram." A justification says "I chose Instagram because 71% of its users are aged 18–34, which matches our target audience of young local customers, directly supporting the business's aim of increasing brand awareness among under-35s in Birmingham."

For every decision ask yourself: "Why was this the RIGHT choice for THIS business?" If you can't answer that — you're describing, not justifying.
✓ Justify your choice of platforms
✓ Justify your target audience identification
✓ Justify your keyword choices
✓ Justify your posting schedule (timing, frequency, content mix)
✓ Justify how your plan meets the specific business requirements

What you're doing: Taking posts you've already made and improving them, showing the before and after.

In plain English: "Optimised" just means "made better." Look at your posts and think — could the image be sharper? Could the caption be more engaging? Could you add a hashtag or a call to action? Change it, screenshot both versions, and explain what you improved and why.

✓ At least 2 posts with before & after screenshots
✓ Annotate what changed and why
✓ Explain how the optimised version better suits the target audience
✓ Show posts tested on at least 2 different devices
🏆 DISTINCTION — Sections 11–12

What you're doing: Writing a full, honest evaluation of your plan and how well it was put into practice.

In plain English: This is your chance to be really honest. What went well? What went badly and why? Did the people who engaged with your posts match the audience you targeted? What would you do completely differently next time? Don't just say "it went well" — give evidence and reasons.

A Distinction evaluation does all five of these:
✓ Judges whether the business requirements were actually met
✓ Explains what worked and what didn't — and WHY
✓ Discusses legal/ethical issues that came up in your actual posts
✓ Links data findings back to your original intentions
✓ Makes specific, justified recommendations for the future

What you're doing: Evaluating your own behaviour and professionalism throughout the whole project.

In plain English: This section is about YOU, not the business. Did you meet your deadlines? How did you react when you got feedback you didn't like? Were you professional when talking to the business owner? Give real examples — vague statements won't earn a Distinction.

Don't say: "I managed my time well."
Do say: "I had planned to complete the posting schedule by 18 March but fell two days behind because I underestimated how long the keyword research would take. I made up the time by working through lunch on 20 March and met the deadline for the business owner meeting."
✓ Time management table with honest reflection
✓ Specific example of responding to feedback
✓ Evidence of professionalism with the business owner
✓ Communication reflection
✓ Creativity and problem-solving examples
Important

Academic Integrity

Read this carefully before you start. It could affect your grade.

✅

You ARE allowed to use AI for:

  • Generating or editing images for your social media posts
  • Helping draft captions or post copy — as long as you review and adapt it yourself
  • Checking grammar or improving your writing
  • Research and brainstorming ideas
❌

You are NOT allowed to:

  • Make up or fabricate analytics data, likes, comments, shares, or reach
  • Use AI to generate fake screenshots of posts or analytics dashboards
  • Invent feedback or meeting notes from the business owner
  • Copy and paste AI-written evaluations, justifications, or reflections as your own work
📊

About your data

Your analytics do not need to be impressive. It does not matter if your post got 1 like or 2 comments — that is your real data and it is perfectly fine to use.

What matters is that your analysis is honest and thoughtful. A post with 1 like that has a strong written analysis is worth far more than fabricated data with a hollow write-up.

⚠️

Consequence of fabricating data

If it is found that you have made up data, fabricated screenshots, or misrepresented your work in any way:

  • Your assignment will be returned for resubmission
  • Your grade will be capped at a Pass — regardless of how good the rest of your work is
